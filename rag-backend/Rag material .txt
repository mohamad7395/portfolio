=== PROJECT: Mill Miner  ===
Predictive Process Optimization for Cold Rolling Mills
SMS Group | Data Science Team | 2022–2025
Project Context and Role
As a member of SMS Group's Data Science team, I worked part-time from 2022 to 2025 on a portfolio of applied data science initiatives supporting the company's cold rolling mill operations. Among these, the Mill Miner project represented the most significant undertaking in terms of scope, technical complexity, and business impact. I worked within a three-person project team under the direct leadership of SMS Group's Data Science Lead, contributing across the full lifecycle of the project, from data acquisition and preprocessing through model development, productization, and deployment.
The project's objective was to identify and quantify optimization opportunities within cold rolling mill processes by analyzing high-frequency raw sensor data captured directly from production-line equipment. The resulting system evolved from an exploratory data analysis effort into a fully deployed, production-grade predictive tool now in active use across SMS Group's cold rolling mills.
Data Engineering and Preprocessing
The foundation of the project was a rigorous data cleaning and transformation pipeline, developed in close collaboration with domain experts through a series of iterative working sessions aimed at aligning our methodology with established industry standards and practical machine behavior.
A central challenge was handling missing values in the sensor data, which we addressed through a context-sensitive strategy rather than a uniform rule. Gaps exceeding one minute were treated as indicative of an underlying equipment issue and excluded from analysis, since interpolating across such intervals risked introducing misleading signal behavior. Shorter gaps, typically a handful of missing rows, were instead treated as transient sensor glitches and resolved through forward-filling from preceding values, preserving continuity in the dataset without compromising its integrity.
Certain signal channels exhibited high levels of noise that interfered with downstream analysis; for these, we applied low-pass filtering to suppress high-frequency noise while retaining the underlying trend relevant to process behavior. Separately, we identified that signal naming conventions were inconsistent across data files, with multiple internal labels referring to the same physical signal. To resolve this, we developed and maintained an external-to-internal name mapping system, ensuring that equivalent signals were correctly unified and consistently referenced throughout the pipeline regardless of their source file.
Data Volume Reduction and Feature Extraction
A defining technical constraint of this project was the sheer scale of the raw data: a single ten-minute window of sensor recordings could reach 10 GB in size. To make this data tractable for both storage and analysis, we developed specialized Python scripts capable of automatically detecting steady-state production phases within the raw signal streams and extracting compact, statistically meaningful summaries of these phases. This approach allowed us to dramatically reduce storage requirements while retaining the essential information needed for downstream modeling, effectively transforming an unmanageable volume of raw telemetry into a structured, analysis-ready dataset.
ETL Pipeline Development
Building on this phase-detection and summarization logic, we engineered a complete ETL (Extract, Transform, Load) pipeline in Python to automate the ingestion and processing of large volumes of production data on a monthly basis. Given the size of individual files, the pipeline was designed with memory efficiency as a core requirement: files were processed in batches of three (each approximately 10 GB), with compact summaries extracted and loaded into a PostgreSQL database before the raw files were cleared from memory. This batching strategy allowed the pipeline to scale reliably without exceeding system memory limits. The entire process was automated and scheduled via cron jobs, enabling unattended, recurring execution.
Statistical Analysis and Custom Predictive Modeling
With a clean, structured dataset in place, we proceeded to formally define the underlying optimization problem in mathematical terms and conducted an in-depth statistical analysis of the data to characterize the search space and the operational behavior of the machinery we sought to model.
This analysis directly informed the design of a custom-built predictive model, which grouped data points according to relevant process features and leveraged distribution quantiles within each group to generate predictions. This approach was tailored specifically to the statistical characteristics of the rolling mill process, rather than relying on generic off-the-shelf modeling techniques. The custom forecasting model substantially outperformed standard approaches, delivering a 30% improvement in predictive performance and capturing 75% of the total theoretically achievable optimization gain identified through our analysis.
From Model to Product: System Architecture and Deployment
Following the success of the predictive modeling work, we transitioned the project from a research prototype into a deployed production system, with real-time thickness prediction as its core functionality.
The system architecture consisted of several interconnected components. The ETL pipeline, described above, was integrated as the automated mechanism for cleaning raw data, segmenting production phases, and populating the PostgreSQL database used for both model training and inference. A separate retraining script was developed to automatically refresh the predictive model on a recurring time-based cycle, ensuring the model remained aligned with evolving production conditions and continued to reflect current plant behavior.
The complete system was deployed on a virtual machine hosted on SMS Group's internal servers, enabling seamless, low-latency connectivity to the factory's control software. Integration with factory systems was implemented end-to-end via gRPC, allowing the system to exchange data with plant control infrastructure in real time. Processed results were persisted in the PostgreSQL database and surfaced through real-time dashboards built with Grafana, giving plant engineers and managers continuous visibility into model outputs and process metrics. A FastAPI-based service layer exposed model predictions and system functionality to downstream consumers, while the modeling components themselves were built using PyTorch, Scikit-learn, and XGBoost, with Pandas and NumPy underpinning the data processing layers throughout.
Operational Impact
The Mill Miner system is now in active, ongoing use at SMS Group's cold rolling mills, where it supports predictive maintenance, process optimization, and transparent, data-driven decision-making for plant personnel. By moving from raw sensor telemetry to actionable, real-time predictions, the system has materially improved the visibility and control that engineers and managers have over mill operations.
Stakeholder Engagement and Project Governance
Over the course of the project's two-year development cycle, our team held regular strategy sessions with stakeholders and senior management. These sessions served to present progress, validate alignment with evolving business requirements, and collaboratively shape the technical roadmap. This sustained engagement with stakeholders was instrumental in ensuring the system's practical adoption on the plant floor and contributed directly to measurable productivity improvements at the operational level.




=== PROJECT: AIME  ===
Agentic Recruitment Platform
DCV Technologies | Lead AI engineer | September 2025 – Present
Project Context and Role
As Lead System Architect and Project Lead at DCV Technologies, I designed and spearheaded the development of AIME (aimetalent.com), an agentic, RAG-driven recruitment ecosystem built to eliminate the friction inherent in global-scale candidate sourcing and matching. The project originated from a defined set of business requirements: DCV needed a central solution capable of sorting incoming candidate resumes and identifying the best-matching candidates for any given job description, at scale and across multiple countries.
I led a team of two developers, owning the full product lifecycle from initial architectural design through to production deployment on AWS EC2. My responsibilities spanned translating stakeholder business requirements into a modular technical architecture, decomposing the project into a concrete implementation roadmap, and overseeing the integration of a multi-layered technology stack involving MongoDB, AWS S3, FAISS, and Gemini File Search Store. Throughout development, I worked directly with non-technical founders and business teams to ensure the platform's technical direction remained aligned with real recruiter workflows and commercial priorities.
System Architecture Overview
The platform is built around two core subsystems: an autonomous Data Engineering and ETL pipeline responsible for ingesting and standardizing candidate data, and an Agentic CV Matcher responsible for high-precision candidate retrieval and ranking at search time. Both subsystems were designed to operate end-to-end without manual intervention, from the moment a CV is uploaded to the moment a ranked candidate list is returned to a recruiter.
Data Ingestion and Geographic Routing
The ingestion pipeline begins with a centralized data lake into which CVs can be uploaded from any source. Rather than storing all candidate data in a single undifferentiated repository, I architected an agentic routing layer that uses an LLM to analyze unstructured CV content — including phone number prefixes and other regional cues drawn from the candidate's most recent position — to autonomously infer the candidate's country of origin. Based on this inference, the system programmatically shards incoming files, routing each CV to a country-specific S3 data lake.
This country-based segmentation extends throughout the entire data architecture: each country maintains its own dedicated S3 bucket for raw CV storage, its own MongoDB collection for structured candidate records, and its own vector database for semantic search. This design allows the platform to scale cleanly across geographies while keeping search operations fast and contextually scoped to the correct regional dataset.
LLM-Based Profile Standardization
Once a CV has been routed to the correct country-specific data lake, a preprocessing layer detects newly arrived files and uses an LLM to parse and standardize their content. Rather than preserving CVs in their original, inconsistent formats, the system extracts structured candidate information and generates a standardized profile for each individual. A key design decision here was to generate dense, meaningful summaries for each job experience — condensing what might otherwise be ten or more disconnected bullet points into a coherent narrative summary — as well as an overall profile summary and a structured skills taxonomy.
Each standardized candidate profile is assigned a unique identifying tag and is dual-written into two destinations: the country-specific vector database, where its content becomes searchable via semantic retrieval, and the corresponding MongoDB collection, where it is stored as structured, queryable candidate data. This ensures that regardless of a CV's original format or language conventions, every candidate in the system becomes uniformly searchable and comparable.
Agentic CV Matching via Retrieval-Augmented Generation
To enable high-precision talent discovery, I engineered an Agentic CV Matcher powered by Retrieval-Augmented Generation. At search time, a recruiter selects the target country to search within and provides a job description. The system automatically extracts key terms and requirements from the job description, then dynamically selects the correct geographic context — the appropriate vector store and MongoDB collection — based on that country selection.
Using this context, the system performs a semantic RAG search to retrieve the most relevant candidate profiles for the given job description. Retrieved candidates are then passed through a re-ranking stage, in which an LLM acts as a judge, scoring each candidate against the job description and explicitly surfacing which keywords from the job description were matched within each candidate's profile. The final output is a ranked shortlist of the top five candidates, each assigned a match score from 0 to 100. Through the platform's front end, recruiters can access either the original uploaded CV or the AI-standardized version derived from the earlier parsing stage, giving them flexibility depending on their review needs.
External Job Board Integration
Beyond matching against internally sourced candidates, I directed the integration of AIME with four external global job boards, including Just Join IT, CV-Library, and Total Jobs. This integration allows the platform to automatically generate and publish job advertisements, and to apply its internal matching and ranking logic to harvest and score candidates sourced externally from these platforms. This effectively transformed AIME from an internal matching tool into a unified, global recruitment intelligence hub capable of sourcing talent from both internal databases and the broader external job market.
Productization, Deployment, and Infrastructure
AIME was fully productized and deployed to a live production environment. I built an automated CI/CD pipeline that packages the application into Docker images and deploys them across both staging and production environments on AWS EC2, ensuring that changes could be tested safely before reaching recruiters actively using the platform. The underlying infrastructure relies on AWS S3 for CV and document storage, MongoDB for structured candidate data, and FAISS-backed vector stores for semantic search, all provisioned within an AWS VPC for network isolation and security.
Operational Impact
AIME is now used daily by hundreds of recruiters, and has reduced a sourcing and matching process that previously took multiple days to a matter of minutes. By combining agentic routing, LLM-based standardization, and retrieval-augmented matching into a single automated pipeline, the platform has materially changed how recruiters at DCV source and evaluate candidates, both from internal databases and external job boards.
Technology Stack
The system was built using RAG, multi-agent workflows, semantic search, MCP, prompt engineering, tool calling, and LLM evaluation as core AI methodologies, implemented with LangChain and LangGraph, and powered by Gemini and OpenAI models. The application layer was developed in Python with FastAPI, using Pandas and NumPy for data processing, MongoDB and FAISS for structured and vector data storage respectively, and deployed on AWS (EC2, S3, VPC) using Docker and CI/CD pipelines with Git-based version control across staging and production environments.



=== PROJECT: Master's Thesis — ABSA LLM Agents ===
Label-Consistent Data Generation for Aspect-Based Sentiment Analysis Using LLM Agents
Master's Thesis | B-IT Institute | Published at EACL 2026
Project Context and Objective
For my master's thesis, I designed and implemented the first agentic data augmentation framework for Aspect-Based Sentiment Analysis (ABSA), a task that requires identifying specific aspect terms within a sentence and correctly classifying the sentiment expressed toward each one. The central motivation behind this work was that ABSA models, particularly lightweight ones, are heavily constrained by the scarcity of high-quality annotated training data, and that existing data augmentation approaches based on naive LLM prompting frequently produce synthetic examples with inconsistent or incorrect labels. My thesis addressed this gap by introducing a structured, agentic generation-and-verification workflow designed explicitly to preserve label consistency in synthetic data.
This work was formalized into a research paper, "Label-Consistent Data Generation for Aspect-Based Sentiment Analysis Using LLM Agents," which was accepted and published at EACL 2026.
Agentic Framework Architecture
The core contribution of the thesis is a two-agent system consisting of a Generator agent and an Evaluator agent, which operate in an iterative loop to produce and validate synthetic training examples.
Generator Agent. The Generator agent is built around two custom tools. The first, a get-policy function, samples a target combination of aspect terms and polarities from the underlying dataset to define what the synthetic example should express. As part of this same function, the agent also randomly retrieves three existing sentences from the dataset and issues an LLM call to characterize their grammar, length, and writing style, ensuring that generated examples remain stylistically consistent with real data. The second tool, generate-sentence, takes the sampled terms, polarities, and style information produced by the get-policy tool and passes them into a predefined prompt template, using an LLM call to produce a candidate synthetic sentence that reflects the sampled labels and style constraints.
Evaluator Agent. Once a candidate sentence is generated, it is passed to the Evaluator agent, which applies two verification tools before accepting the example. The first tool performs a surface-level check to confirm that the sampled aspect terms actually appear in the generated string, which is necessary because LLM generation frequently alters terms during phrasing, for instance converting plural forms to singular or introducing minor spelling variations. The second, more rigorous tool performs the ABSA task itself on the generated sentence, verifying not only that the sampled aspect terms appear in the correct grammatical role as genuine aspect terms, but also that they carry exactly the polarity originally sampled. Only if both checks pass does the Evaluator agent write the example to disk; if either check fails, the pipeline loops back and invokes the Generator agent again to produce a new candidate.
Prompting-Based Baseline
To rigorously isolate the contribution of the agentic architecture itself, I designed a closely matched prompting-based baseline pipeline that reused the exact same underlying models and prompt templates as the agentic system, but without any iteration or self-verification: each prompt was issued once, and the model's first response was accepted and saved directly as a training example, with no generator-evaluator loop. This design ensured that any performance differences observed between the two pipelines could be attributed specifically to the agentic generation-and-verification structure rather than to differences in prompt content or model choice.
Experimental Design and Evaluation
The framework was evaluated across three core ABSA subtasks: Aspect Term Extraction (ATE), Aspect Sentiment Classification (ATSC), and Aspect Sentiment Pair Extraction (ASPE). Evaluation was conducted on four SemEval benchmark datasets, using two instruction-tuned encoder-decoder models, T5-Base and Tk-Instruct, allowing me to study how the benefits of agentic augmentation varied across model scale and pretraining strategy.
I designed a systematic experimentation framework to study how downstream performance was affected by task complexity, tuning strategy, and the ratio of synthetic to real training data, including comparisons between 1:1 and 2:1 ratios of synthetic-to-real data across each of the three subtasks and both models.
Key Findings and Contributions
The evaluation demonstrated that the agentic workflow consistently outperformed naive prompting-based generation, achieving approximately a 6% improvement in F1-score, primarily driven by higher label consistency and reduced noise in the generated examples. This advantage was most pronounced on tasks requiring aspect term generation, while gains were more limited on the structurally more complex ASPE task, which requires joint prediction of both term and polarity.
When agentic synthetic data was mixed with real training data, it produced consistently higher performance gains than prompting-based synthetic data, with benefits varying significantly by model. These improvements were strongest for T5-Base, where augmentation with agentic data allowed the smaller model to approach, and in some configurations match, the performance of the more extensively pretrained Tk-Instruct model, effectively narrowing the performance gap normally attributable to large-scale instruction tuning. Tk-Instruct, already benefiting from more extensive pretraining, showed comparatively smaller improvements from augmentation. In low-resource scenarios, agentic augmentation was shown to produce results competitive with those obtained from real benchmark datasets.
The experiments further revealed that augmentation was only beneficial in moderation: a 1:1 mixing ratio of synthetic-to-real data yielded consistent improvements, while larger volumes of synthetic data diluted overall data quality and reduced downstream performance, indicating a practical limit to how much synthetic augmentation should be introduced relative to real annotated data.
Contribution Summary
This thesis introduced the first agentic data augmentation framework specifically designed for ABSA, integrating multi-step generation and self-reflection through a Generator-Evaluator (LLM-as-judge) workflow to ensure the label consistency and linguistic quality of synthetic training data. It closed part of the performance gap between lightweight models and state-of-the-art systems by introducing high-quality, label-consistent synthetic data, and established a systematic experimentation framework for studying how task complexity, tuning strategy, and data mixing ratios affect downstream ABSA performance. The work demonstrated that agentic augmentation outperforms naive prompting by approximately 6% F1, and that in low-resource conditions, it can produce results competitive with real benchmark data.
Publication
This research was published at EACL 2026 under the title "Label-Consistent Data Generation for Aspect-Based Sentiment Analysis Using LLM Agents" (https://arxiv.org/pdf/2602.16379). The published abstract summarizes the work as an agentic data augmentation method for ABSA that uses iterative generation and verification to produce high-quality synthetic training examples, evaluated against a closely matched prompting-based baseline across three ABSA subtasks, four SemEval datasets, and two encoder-decoder models. The paper's conclusions emphasize that controlled, agentic generation offers a practical and scalable strategy for enhancing ABSA models without requiring additional pretraining or human annotation, particularly benefiting lightweight models such as T5-Base and demonstrating that gains are task-dependent and effective primarily in moderate, rather than large-scale, augmentation ratios.
Technology Stack
The framework was implemented in Python, using LangChain and LangGraph to orchestrate the multi-agent generator-evaluator workflow, with Hugging Face and Ollama used for model access and inference. Core NLP processing relied on spaCy and NLTK, with Pandas used for data handling throughout the experimentation pipeline. The methodology drew on multi-agent workflows and agentic orchestration, prompt engineering, tool calling, and both LLM-based and standard model evaluation techniques.
